# Fokker–Planck Equation

> The Fokker–Planck equation, also known as the Kolmogorov forward equation, is the partial‑differential equation that governs the time evolution of the probability density function of a stochastic process described by an Itô stochastic differential equation. It expresses how drift and diffusion (deterministic dynamics and random perturbations) transport and spread the probability density over the state space. In the deterministic, diffusion‑less case it reduces to a pure advection equation.

## The Equation

The evolution of the probability density function (PDF) of a stochastic state $\mathbf{x}\in\mathbb{R}^{2n}$ governed by an Itô stochastic differential equation;[^ito]

$$
d\mathbf{x}(t)=\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x},t) dt + \mathbf{G}(\mathbf{x},t) d\mathbf{W}(t)
$$

is described by the Fokker–Planck equation:

$$
\boxed{
\frac{\partial \mathcal{P}}{\partial t}
= -\nabla_{\mathbf{x}}\!\cdot\!\bigl(\mathbf{f}\mathcal{P}\bigr)
   +\frac{1}{2}\,\nabla_{\mathbf{x}}\!\cdot\!
   \bigl(\mathbf{Q}\nabla_{\mathbf{x}} \mathcal{P}\bigr)
}
$$

where $\mathcal{P}(\mathbf{x},t)$ is the PDF and $\mathbf{Q}(\mathbf{x},t)=\mathbf{G}(\mathbf{x},t)\,\mathbf{G}^{\mathsf{T}}(\mathbf{x},t)$ is the diffusion matrix.  

## Diffusion‑less Hamiltonian System

For a deterministic Hamiltonian system (no stochastic acceleration, i.e., $\mathbf{G}= \mathbf{0} \Rightarrow \mathbf{Q}= \mathbf{0}$) the Fokker–Planck equation reduces to a pure transport equation:[^transport]

$$
\frac{\partial \mathcal{P}}{\partial t}
= -\nabla_{\mathbf{x}}\!\cdot\!\bigl(\mathbf{f}\mathcal{P}\bigr)
$$

with the Hamiltonian dynamics,

$$
\begin{align*}
&\mathbf{x} = \begin{pmatrix}\mathbf{q}\\ \mathbf{p}\end{pmatrix}  \\
%%%%%
&\mathbf{J} = \begin{pmatrix} \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{I}\\ -\mathbf{I} & \mathbf{0} \end{pmatrix}  \\
%%%%%
&\dot{\mathbf{x}} = \mathbf{J}\nabla_{\mathbf{x}} H(\mathbf{x},t) 
\end{align*}
$$

where $\mathbf{q}$ is the generalized coordinates (position), $\mathbf{p}$ is the generalized momenta (velocity), $H(\mathbf{q},\mathbf{p},t)$ is the Hamiltonian (energy) function, $\mathbf{I}$ is an $n\times n$ identity matrix, and $\mathbf{0}$ is an $n\times n$ zero matrix.  

Because the flow generated by a Hamiltonian system is *incompressible*, the PDF satisfies the integral invariance;

$$
\begin{align*}
&1 = \int_{\mathbb{R}^{2n}} \mathcal{P}(\mathbf{x},t) \, d\mathbf{x}  \\
%%%%%
&0 = \frac{d}{dt}\int_{\mathcal{V}(t)} \mathcal{P} \, d\mathbf{x}
\end{align*}
$$

where $\mathcal{V}(t)$ is any volume advected by the deterministic flow.



[^ito]:  An Itô stochastic differential equation (SDE) describes the time evolution of a random state $x(t)$ as a combination of deterministic dynamics and stochastic perturbations. In Itô form it is written: $$dx(t)=f\bigl(x(t),t\bigr)dt + G\bigl(x(t),t\bigr)dW(t)$$ where $f(x,t)$  is the drift term (the ordinary differential equation part), $G(x,t)$ is the diffusion matrix that scales the random input, and $dW(t)$ is differential of a Wiener process (standard Brownian motion), representing white‑noise increments.  The equation specifies how the state changes instantaneously, with the first term giving a smooth, deterministic flow and the second term adding random fluctuations. The associated probability density of $x(t)$ evolves according to the Kolmogorov forward equation.

[^transport]: A transport equation (also called an advection equation) is a partial‑differential equation that describes how a quantity $u(\mathbf{x},t)$ is carried or moved by a flow field $\mathbf{v}(\mathbf{x},t)$. In its simplest form: $$\frac{\partial u}{\partial t} + \nabla_{\mathbf{x}}\!\cdot\!\bigl(\mathbf{v}u\bigr)=0$$ When the diffusion term in the Fokker–Planck equation is absent, the equation reduces to this transport form, meaning the probability density is simply advected by the deterministic dynamics without spreading.
